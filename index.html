What is a Cookie?

An HTTP cookie is a tiny piece of data a website stores in your browser. Think of it as a loyalty card — every time you come back, the site recognizes you by reading that card.

🏪 Loyalty Card Analogy

Like a coffee shop stamp card — the server gives you a token, you bring it back next visit.

Without cookies, every page visit is a fresh start — the server has no memory of you. Cookies solve the "statelessness" problem of HTTP.
🛂 Visitor Badge Analogy

Like a badge at a conference — you get it at the door and show it at every station.

The badge (cookie) proves who you are without you having to re-register at every room. But what if someone steals your badge?

🔬 Cookie Anatomy — Click any field to learn more

Name
=
sessionID
Unique identifier key
📌 The name is how the server identifies which cookie to read. Like the label on a jar. Common names: session_id, user_token, _ga (Google Analytics), fbp (Facebook Pixel).
Value
=
a4f8c2e91b...
The actual stored data
🔐 The value is the actual data. Often a random token or encoded user ID. This is what gets sent back to the server with every request.
Domain
=
.example.com
Which domain owns it
🌐 The domain controls which server receives the cookie. A leading dot (e.g. .example.com) means ALL subdomains. Third-party cookies have a different domain than the page you're on!
Path
=
/
URL scope for the cookie
📁 The path restricts the cookie to certain URLs. / means the whole site. /cart means only cart pages get the cookie.
Expires
=
2025-12-31T00:00Z
When it gets deleted
⏰ Expiry determines the cookie's lifespan. No expiry = session cookie (deleted when browser closes). Long expiry = persistent cookie that survives reboots. Some trackers set expiry years into the future!
Flags
=
Secure; HttpOnly; SameSite=Lax
Security attributes
🛡️ Flags add security rules:
• Secure → only send over HTTPS
• HttpOnly → JavaScript cannot read this cookie (XSS protection)
• SameSite=Strict/Lax/None → controls cross-site behavior (CSRF protection)

💻 Real Cookie Example

HTTP Response Header 
# Server sends this when you log in:
Set-Cookie: sessionID=a4f8c2e91b3d7f6a; 
           Domain=.mysite.com; 
           Path=/; 
           Expires=Wed, 31 Dec 2025 23:59:59 GMT; 
           Secure; HttpOnly; SameSite=Lax

# Your browser sends this cookie with EVERY request to mysite.com:
GET /profile HTTP/1.1
Host: mysite.com
Cookie: sessionID=a4f8c2e91b3d7f6a

⏳ Session Cookies

Deleted automatically when you close your browser tab or window. They live in memory, not on disk. Used for: shopping cart contents, login state during a single visit.

✅
Privacy friendly: No trace left after browsing. Great for temporary states.
💾 Persistent Cookies

Survive browser restarts. Stored on your disk until the expiry date or manually deleted. Used for: "Remember Me" login, preferences, and most tracking.

⚠️
Watch out: Trackers set expiry years in the future. Some cookies set in 2020 may still be on your device!
Cookie
# Expires 2 years from now = persistent tracker
Set-Cookie: _track=uid_99182; Expires=Fri, 01 Jan 2027 00:00:00 GMT
🏠 First-Party Cookies

Set by the website you're visiting (same domain). Essential for features like login, cart, and preferences. Generally considered legitimate and necessary.

ℹ️
Even first-party analytics can be invasive. A site may send your data to Google Analytics — that's still tracking you.
👁️ Third-Party Cookies

Set by a different domain than the one you're on. A tracker embedded on 10,000 sites can follow your journey across all of them. This is the backbone of online advertising.

⚠️
The big privacy threat. Chrome is phasing them out. Firefox and Safari already block them by default.
🔒 Secure Cookies

Only transmitted over encrypted HTTPS connections. Prevents cookies from being intercepted on public WiFi (man-in-the-middle attacks).

Cookie Flag
Set-Cookie: token=xyz; Secure
# Only sent over https:// — never http://
🚫 HttpOnly Cookies

JavaScript cannot read these cookies. Protects against XSS attacks where malicious scripts try to steal your session cookie.

JavaScript Test
// Try to steal the cookie with JS:
console.log(document.cookie)
// Result: "" — empty! HttpOnly hides it from JS
// Without HttpOnly: "sessionID=a4f8c2e91b..." — STOLEN!

How Online Tracking Works

Click each step of the journey to see exactly what happens behind the scenes when you browse the web.

🌐
1. You Visit a Website
Your browser sends a GET request. The server sees your IP, browser, OS, timezone...
GET / HTTP/1.1 Host: news-site.com User-Agent: Mozilla/5.0 (iPhone; CPU iPhone OS 17_0) Accept-Language: en-US # Server already knows: iPhone user, US, English, iOS 17
🍪
2. Trackers Drop Their Cookies
The site loads 3rd-party ads, widgets, analytics. Each one sets their own cookie.
Set-Cookie: _ga=GA1.2.1234567890; Domain=.google-analytics.com; Expires=2026-01-01 Set-Cookie: _fbp=fb.1.12345.678; Domain=.facebook.com; Expires=2026-01-01 Set-Cookie: uid=ad-tracker-99182; Domain=.doubleclick.net # 3 trackers from 1 page visit!
🛍️
3. You Browse Other Sites
You visit a shopping site, a news site, a forum. Each loads the same trackers.
# Same tracker cookie sent from shopping.com to doubleclick.net: Cookie: uid=ad-tracker-99182 # Tracker now knows: # - You visited: news.com, shopping.com, forum.com # - At 8pm on a Tuesday # - From New York # - On an iPhone
🎯
4. Your Profile is Built
The tracker aggregates all visits into a behavioral profile sold to advertisers.
{ "uid": "ad-tracker-99182", "interests": ["technology", "shoes", "sports"], "income_estimate": "high", "location": "New York", "device": "iPhone", "active_hours": "evening", "profile_completeness": "87%" }
📢
5. Targeted Ads Follow You
That shoe you looked at appears everywhere. This is behavioral retargeting.
# Real-time bidding happens in <100ms: # 1. You load a page with ad slot # 2. Ad exchange auctions your profile # 3. Nike bids $0.0023 for your eyeballs # 4. Nike's shoe ad wins and loads # ALL before the page finishes loading!

🔍 What is a Tracking Pixel?

A tracking pixel is a 1×1 invisible image embedded in emails and web pages. When your browser loads it, it pings the tracker's server — confirming you opened the email.

HTML — Tracking Pixel
<!-- Hidden in an email HTML — totally invisible -->
<img 
  src="https://tracker.com/pixel.gif?uid=99182&email=you@gmail.com"
  width="1" height="1"
  style="display:none"
/>

<!-- When you open the email, your IP + timestamp logged -->
<!-- Tracker now knows: opened at 8:03pm, New York IP -->

🖐️ Browser Fingerprinting

By combining dozens of browser attributes, sites create a unique "fingerprint" that identifies you — even without any cookies. It's like identifying someone by their voice rather than their ID card.

JavaScript — Fingerprint Collection
// Trackers collect all this from your browser:
const fingerprint = {
  userAgent: navigator.userAgent,     // Browser + OS version
  language:  navigator.language,       // "en-US"
  timezone:  Intl.DateTimeFormat().resolvedOptions().timeZone,
  screen:    `${screen.width}x${screen.height}`,
  plugins:   [...navigator.plugins].map(p => p.name),
  fonts:     // detect via canvas/CSS tricks
  canvas:    // pixel-level rendering differences
  webgl:     // GPU signature
}
// Hash all of it → unique ID for YOUR device
🚨
Incognito mode doesn't help. Your fingerprint is the same in private mode. This is why fingerprinting is considered more invasive than cookies.
🗄️ Local Storage / Session Storage

Web Storage APIs allow sites to store much more data than cookies — and localStorage never expires. Unlike cookies, they're not automatically sent to the server, but JavaScript can read and use them for tracking.

JavaScript
// localStorage: persists until manually cleared
localStorage.setItem('trackingID', 'uid-99182-permanent')

// sessionStorage: cleared when tab closes
sessionStorage.setItem('visit', '1')

// localStorage has 5-10MB limit vs 4KB for cookies
// Can store full behavioral history offline!
🔬 Tracking Pixels in Email

Marketing emails embed invisible 1×1 images. When your email client loads the image, it logs: your IP address, email client, device, exact open time — all without your knowledge or consent.

📧
To protect yourself: Disable "Load Remote Images" in your email client. Gmail and Apple Mail now pre-load images through their own proxy to mask your IP.
🧟 Supercookies / Evercookies

Evercookies store tracking data in multiple locations simultaneously. Even if you delete cookies, the script recreates them from the surviving copies. True zombie behavior.

Storage Locations Used
// An evercookie stores your ID in ALL of these:
[
  "HTTP Cookies",       // Standard cookies
  "localStorage",        // Web storage
  "IndexedDB",           // Browser database
  "Cache API",           // Service worker cache
  "ETag Headers",        // HTTP caching mechanism
  "HSTS Supercookie",    // HTTPS preloading exploit
  "Canvas Fingerprint"  // No storage needed!
]
// Delete ANY of them → restored from the rest

📢 Advertisers & Ad Networks

Ad networks like Google Ads and DoubleClick are embedded on millions of websites. They track which sites you visit, what you search, what you buy — then sell ad slots targeted to you specifically via real-time bidding in under 100ms.

💡
Google's ad network reaches over 35 million websites. If you visit any of them, Google may track you — even if you never visit google.com.
📊 Analytics Platforms

Google Analytics (GA) is on ~56% of all websites. While its stated purpose is helping site owners understand traffic, it also sends behavioral data back to Google. A more privacy-friendly alternative: Plausible.io or Matomo.

GA Cookie
# Google Analytics sets this on almost every site:
_ga=GA1.2.1234567890.1680000000
# Unique client ID — tracks you across all GA sites
# Expires: 2 years
👍 Social Media Trackers

The Facebook Pixel is embedded on millions of e-commerce sites. It tracks what you view and buy — even if you're logged out of Facebook. This data feeds Meta's ad targeting system and is sent back to Meta's servers.

🚨
In 2022, the FTC found hospitals sharing sensitive patient data with Meta via the Facebook Pixel — including health conditions and appointment details.
🏦 Data Brokers

Companies like Acxiom, Experian, and LexisNexis collect data from thousands of sources — websites, public records, purchase histories, app usage — and sell detailed profiles to anyone willing to pay. Your profile may include income estimates, political views, and health predictions.

📋
Under GDPR and CCPA you have the right to request and delete your data. Sites like DeleteMe automate opt-out requests to hundreds of brokers.

Privacy Laws & Regulations

Governments have responded to the tracking crisis with legislation. Click each law to explore what it means for you.

🇪🇺 GDPR — General Data Protection Regulation

Europe's landmark privacy law — the gold standard worldwide. Click to expand.

📅
In effect: May 25, 2018
🌍
Scope: Any site serving EU users
💰
Fine: Up to €20M or 4% global revenue

Your Rights Under GDPR:

  • Right to access your data
  • Right to be forgotten (erasure)
  • Right to data portability
  • Right to object to processing
  • Explicit consent required before tracking
🇺🇸 CCPA — California Consumer Privacy Act

California's privacy law, similar to GDPR but with US flavor. Click to expand.

📅
In effect: January 1, 2020
🏢
Scope: CA residents + companies $25M+ revenue
💰
Fine: $2,500–$7,500 per violation

Key Rights:

  • Know what data is collected
  • Opt-out of data sales ("Do Not Sell My Info")
  • Delete personal data on request
  • Non-discrimination for exercising rights
🍪 Cookie Consent Banners — Why Do They Exist?

GDPR requires informed, explicit consent before setting non-essential cookies. That's why every site has a banner. Click to learn what your choices actually mean.

⚠️
"Accept All": You consent to ALL tracking — advertising, analytics, social media pixels, and data sharing with hundreds of third parties.
✅
"Reject Non-Essential": Only strictly necessary cookies (login, cart) are allowed. All tracking blocked. Usually the better choice.
🎨
Dark Patterns: Many sites design the "Reject" button to be small, hidden, or require multiple clicks — while "Accept All" is bright and prominent. This is called a "dark pattern" and is illegal under GDPR.

How to Protect Your Privacy

Click each practice to mark it as done — build your privacy checklist!

🔒
Always use HTTPS sites
Look for the padlock icon. HTTPS encrypts your traffic and prevents cookie theft on public WiFi.
🦊
Use Firefox or Brave browser
Both block third-party cookies and fingerprinting by default. Brave also blocks ads automatically.
🧩
Install uBlock Origin extension
The most effective ad and tracker blocker. Free, open-source, blocks thousands of trackers.
🗑️
Clear cookies regularly
Set your browser to clear cookies on exit, or manually clear them weekly. Erases stored profiles.
🕵️
Use private/incognito mode
Doesn't store cookies between sessions. Remember: fingerprinting still works in incognito!
❌
Reject non-essential cookies
Always click "Reject" or "Only Necessary" on cookie banners. Don't click "Accept All".
📧
Disable remote images in email
Blocks tracking pixels from loading. Available in Gmail, Outlook, and Apple Mail settings.
🌐
Use a VPN
Hides your real IP address from trackers. Combined with other measures, significantly reduces tracking.
